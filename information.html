Usage of Recorded Crime and Survey Data

Recorded crime data

Surveys on their own should not be used to monitor and measure prevalence and the recommended approach is to use police recorded crime and survey data together to measure prevalence. Both police recorded crime and population level surveys have strengths and limitations but used together, will indicate a valuable baseline of VAWG in NI.

Police recorded crime statistics do not include crimes that are not reported to the police or that are not included in the Home Office notifiable offence list but they include a wider range of crime types than Survey data, e.g. homicide, crimes against children, crimes against businesses and many, so-called 'victimless' crimes (such as drug possession offences).

The number of crimes recorded by the police can be affected by the rules governing the recording of the data, systems in place, operational decisions in respect of the allocation of resources and circumstances that may have an impact on willingness to report to police.

• Gender: Refer to gender and not sex, as gender is what is recorded. The percentages male/female may not add to 100% as totals may include gender unknown – Gender unknown may include persons who have not identified as either male or female.
• Victims: Crimes can be committed against people, businesses/organisations and some crimes are state-based. They represent each time a person, business or organisation reports being a victim of crime to police, regardless of how many times they may make such a report. The figures presented in the dashboard focus only on those crimes with a person victim.
• Crime recording date: Crimes are recorded based on the date the report was made to police, regardless of when they actually occurred. On many occasions crimes both occur and are reported on the same day. However certain crime types, particularly sexual offences, have a higher proportion of reports that are ‘non-recent’ or historic, in that there may be a large time gap between occurrence and report.
• What crimes are recorded? Where a person has been subject to more than one offence in a single report made to police, it is generally the case that only the most serious crime is recorded (as per Home Office Counting Rules guidance). For example: if a person is assaulted and their car is damaged, only the assault will be recorded.

Survey data

Surveys may shed light on the large proportion of crime not reported to the police and the data collected are unaffected by police reporting and recording practices, however survey data coverage is usually restricted to non-fatal crimes against private households and their adult occupants (it excludes, for instance, homicide, crimes against children, fraud, crimes against businesses, organised crime and so-called 'victimless' crimes);.

There are generally methodological consistencies between survey years although some surveys (or modules within) are not carried out annually. Survey data has the potential to become a more reliable device for measuring trends for crimes against households and their adult occupants. However, it doesn't facilitate local crime pattern analysis and much larger sample sizes would be required to facilitate the tracking of small, statistically significant changes in the levels of particular types of crime. Survey data is also subject to sampling and non-sampling errors.

Additional considerations

It is also worth noting that police recorded crime figures cannot, by their nature, provide an impression of the extent of concern about crime (often described as 'fear of crime') among different sections of the community. For the crime types it covers, surveys provide a more complete measure of the extent and impact of crime against private households and their adult occupants, so it is necessary to complement police figures with information drawn from surveys.

As exemplified above, police recorded crime and survey data have different strengths and limitations but can be looked at together to provide a more comprehensive picture of prevalence of gender-based violence than could be obtained from either series alone.
